Slide 1: Title Page (~30s) [Speaker: Aaron] "Hi everyone, I'm Aaron, and I’ Sherlock. Today, we are excited to present our Final Project: AC-DPO, which stands for Adaptive Capacity Direct Preference Optimization. We are introducing a new way to align language models by dynamically matching the model's computational capacity to the difficulty of the training data."
Slide 2: The Problem (~45s) [Speaker: Aaron] "Let's start with the problem: static alignment scaling. Normally, standard DPO trains a model using a fixed LoRA rank—like r=64—for all data types. But preference data isn't uniform. When you use massive capacity on 'Easy' data, it wastes compute and actually causes the model to overfit, learning superficial formatting instead of actual logic. Conversely, if you use a low rank, the model underfits and struggles to capture the complex reasoning required for 'Hard' pairs. This capacity mismatch is the core bottleneck we want to solve."
Slide 3: Research Question (~30s) [Speaker: Aaron] "This leads us directly to our core research question. Can dynamically increasing the LoRA rank during a curriculum-based DPO process preserve, or even increase, the performance of a fixed high-rank baseline? And crucially, can we do this while significantly reducing the trainable capacity used in the early stages of training? We hypothesized that the answer is yes."
Slide 4: Our Solution - AC-DPO (~50s) [Speaker: Aaron] "To answer this, we developed AC-DPO. Inspired by Curry-DPO’s curriculum approach and Curriculum DPO++’s dynamic LoRA approach not regarding to LLM. Instead of a fixed capacity, we use a dynamic, two-stage expansion. In Stage 1, focusing on efficiency, we feed the model 'Easy' data and restrict it to a low-rank adapter of r=8. This forces the model to learn basic style and formatting quickly. Then comes the pivotal step: we permanently merge these r=8 weights into the base model. Finally, in Stage 2, we initialize a high-capacity r=64 adapter specifically for the 'Hard' dataset. This dedicates the model's full capacity entirely to complex reasoning."
Slide 5: Implementation (~45s) [Speaker: Sherlock] "To test this, we used a 10,000-pair subset from the Anthropic RLHF dataset. We used a DeBERTa reward model to score pairs and partition them into an 'Easy' bucket for low-entropy signals and a 'Hard' bucket for complex reasoning. To ensure a strictly fair evaluation against the baseline, we locked in our fairness controls: exactly 4 training epochs, a fixed batch size of 4, and a constant random seed of 5100."
Slide 6: Key Results (~50s) (Take your time here, point to the specific metrics on the screen) [Speaker: Sherlock] "So, what did we find? On Hard Tasks, AC-DPO successfully achieved superior accuracy. We saw clear logic and reasoning gains, hitting 51.8% overall accuracy and 52.6% fine-grained token accuracy, both beating the baseline. By saving high capacity for hard logic, the model makes sharper vocabulary choices. However, we did observe a limitation on the Easy Tasks. Performance showed a slight regression—for instance, 34.7% accuracy compared to the baseline's 35.4%. This drop is the direct result of our dynamic capacity allocation prioritizing difficult logic over basic tasks."
Slide 7: Conclusion & Future Work (~40s) [Speaker: Sherlock] "To conclude, AC-DPO represents a strategic trade-off between performance and efficiency. We win on complex logic and secure significant compute savings early on, achieving near statistical parity overall at 43.25% versus the baseline's 43.50%—despite using far fewer total FLOPs. The slight regression on easy tasks reveals a 'capacity ceiling,' where r=8 is just too constrained for large-scale easy data. For our next steps, we plan to increase the initial rank on a production-level model and implement 'Experience Replay' to prevent the model from forgetting basic alignments. Thank you for listening, and we'd be happy to take any questions."
